## Spontaneous vs. Responsive Desire: What’s the Difference?

Ever wonder why you don’t feel “in the mood” as often as you think you should? The way we experience sexual desire is often misunderstood. Let’s explore what they are and why one might feel more familiar to you.

### What Is Spontaneous Desire?

Spontaneous desire is that sudden, out-of-the-blue urge to have sex. It’s the classic “lightning bolt” of passion that strikes without any specific trigger. You might be working, reading, or just going about your day when the thought of being intimate pops into your head and feels exciting. This is the type of desire most often portrayed in pop culture, which can set an unrealistic standard. While it’s a valid way to experience desire, it’s not the only way. If you find this doesn’t happen to you often, you’re not alone, and it doesn’t necessarily mean you have a [low libido](/low-libido-and-your-relationships/).

Responsive desire, on the other hand, is desire that follows arousal. Instead of starting with a mental urge for sex, it begins with physical or emotional stimulation. Think of it as your body getting the memo before your brain does. A loving touch, an intimate conversation, or a romantic setting can create the initial spark of arousal, and desire follows. For many women, the brain is the most important sex organ, and desire is deeply connected to feelings and context. So much of our day is spent in our heads, worrying about the past or planning for the future. A simple practice is to take a few moments to tune into your physical senses. Focus on the feeling of your feet on the floor, the texture of your clothes, or the taste of your morning coffee. This practice of bringing your attention to the present moment can help quiet mental chatter and make it easier to connect with your body’s signals, including those related to arousal.
