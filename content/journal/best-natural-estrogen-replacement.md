## What Are Natural Estrogen Replacements?

When we talk about “natural” estrogen replacements, we’re usually referring to phytoestrogens. These are compounds found in plants that have a structure similar to the estrogen our bodies produce. Because of this similarity, they can interact with our body’s estrogen receptors and create mild estrogen-like effects. Think of them as nature’s way of offering a gentle hormonal nudge, which can be especially helpful when your own estrogen levels are fluctuating during perimenopause and menopause.

You can find these helpful compounds in a variety of everyday foods. Soy products, flaxseed, lentils, and chickpeas are some of the most well-known sources. By simply incorporating more of these foods into your meals, you may find some relief from common symptoms tied to low estrogen, like hot flashes or night sweats. While dietary changes can be a great first step in managing your hormonal health, they are often one piece of a larger puzzle. For more persistent or severe symptoms, a personalized treatment plan, such as [Bioidentical Hormone Replacement Therapy](/biote-hormone-therapy/), can offer more comprehensive support.

### How Phytoestrogens Work in Your Body

So, how exactly do these plant compounds work? Phytoestrogens, like the isoflavones in soy or the lignans in flaxseed oil, are able to bind to the estrogen receptors throughout your body. Because they are much weaker than the estrogen your body makes, their effect is subtle. Research suggests they can offer mild relief from menopause symptoms, particularly hot flashes and night sweats, while also potentially supporting your bone density and heart health. This gentle action is why many women turn to [phytoestrogen-rich foods](https://www.uclahealth.org/news/article/how-increase-estrogen-naturally) as a natural starting point for managing their symptoms and supporting their overall wellness.

### The Two Main Types: Isoflavones and Lignans

Phytoestrogens generally fall into two main categories, and knowing the difference can help you make more intentional food choices.

First, we have **isoflavones**. These are found in high concentrations in soy products like tofu, edamame, and soy milk. Isoflavones are particularly well-studied for their ability to help reduce the frequency and intensity of hot flashes and night sweats, which are common complaints during the menopausal transition.

The second type is **lignans**. Flaxseed is the richest dietary source of lignans, though you can also find them in other seeds, grains, and vegetables. Lignans play a key role in helping to balance hormone levels and can be especially useful for easing symptoms during perimenopause.
