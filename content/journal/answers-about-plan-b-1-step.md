When normal methods of birth control fail, it’s best to have an emergency backup ready at hand. Enter Plan B: the emergency contraception pill, also known as the “morning after” pill. If a condom breaks or you’re otherwise worried about unprotected sex, Plan B will prevent 75-89% of unplanned pregnancies if taken in the three days after sex.

## Is It Safe to Take Plan B?

Plan B works by releasing a hormone called levonorgestrel, a lab-made version of progesterone, into your body to delay ovulation and prevent implantation of any eggs into the uterine wall. It may have mild side effects such as an upset stomach or dizziness, but the most common is a change in your period, which may become lighter or heavier, or occur off its normal schedule. Though Plan B should not be used regularly or long-term, it is perfectly safe for occasional pregnancy prevention. ### How Long Do You Have to Take Plan B?

When it comes to taking Plan B, the sooner, the better. For the highest level of effectiveness, you should take Plan B One-Step within 72 hours (three days) of unprotected sex. Acting within this timeframe gives you the best chance of preventing an unplanned pregnancy. However, you do have a slightly larger window than many people realize. According to [Yale Health](https://yalehealth.yale.edu/topic/emergency-contraception-morning-after-pill), emergency contraception can still be taken up to 120 hours (five days) after intercourse. It’s crucial to remember that its effectiveness decreases with each day that passes. If you find yourself needing guidance on contraception or have questions about your options, speaking with a women’s health expert can provide clarity and support for your specific needs.

## Where to Get Emergency Contraception

When you need emergency contraception, time is of the essence. Fortunately, you have a few reliable options for getting it quickly. The most common choice is purchasing it over-the-counter at your local pharmacy or drugstore. Most major chains carry Plan B or its generic equivalents, and you don’t need a prescription to buy it. However, if you’re looking for guidance or want to discuss other options, seeking professional medical advice is a great step. Knowing where to turn can give you peace of mind and ensure you get the care you need without delay, allowing you to make an informed decision about your health in a timely manner.

## Beyond the Morning-After Pill: Other Birth Control Options

While emergency contraception is a crucial tool, it’s designed for occasional use, not as a primary method of birth control. Thinking about a long-term strategy can give you more control and peace of mind. There are many different types of birth control available today, each with its own set of benefits and considerations. From daily pills to long-acting devices, the right option for you depends on your lifestyle, health history, and personal preferences. Exploring these choices with a healthcare provider can help you find a method that fits seamlessly into your life and empowers you to manage your reproductive health proactively.

### Barrier Methods

Barrier methods are a straightforward and hormone-free way to prevent pregnancy. As explained by [Essex County OB/GYN](https://ecobgyn.org/services/birth-control/), these methods work by creating a physical barrier that stops sperm from reaching an egg. The most common examples are male and female condoms, diaphragms, and contraceptive sponges. Condoms also have the added benefit of protecting against sexually transmitted infections (STIs). Barrier methods are used only during sex, making them a good option for people who prefer not to use a daily or long-term method. They are widely available and can be a reliable choice when used correctly and consistently.

### Hormonal Methods

Hormonal methods are a popular and highly effective way to prevent pregnancy. These options, which include birth control pills, patches, and shots, use hormones to stop ovulation so there’s no egg to be fertilized. Many women also appreciate the additional benefits of hormonal birth control, such as lighter periods and clearer skin. However, they require consistency—a pill must be taken daily, and patches or shots need to be administered on a strict schedule. Discussing your health history with a doctor is important to ensure a hormonal method is a safe and suitable choice for your body and lifestyle.

### Long-Acting Reversible Contraceptives (LARCs)

For those seeking a “set it and forget it” approach, Long-Acting Reversible Contraceptives (LARCs) are an excellent choice. These include devices like IUDs (Mirena, Kyleena, Paragard) and implantable rods like Nexplanon. Once placed by a healthcare provider, LARCs can prevent pregnancy for several years, making them one of the most effective forms of birth control available. They are reversible, so your fertility returns to normal soon after the device is removed. This combination of convenience and effectiveness makes LARCs a top choice for many women who want reliable, long-term protection without a daily routine.

### Permanent Birth Control

If you are certain that you do not want to have children in the future, permanent birth control may be the right path for you. These methods, such as tubal ligation for women, are surgical procedures that permanently prevent pregnancy. This is a significant decision that should be made after careful consideration and thorough discussion with your partner and a trusted healthcare provider. While highly effective, it’s important to remember that these procedures are generally not reversible, so they are best suited for individuals who are confident in their decision to prevent pregnancy for the rest of their lives.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Is the morning-after pill the same as the abortion pill?** This is a common point of confusion, but they are two completely different medications. The morning-after pill, like Plan B, works by preventing or delaying ovulation to stop a pregnancy from ever starting. It will not end an existing pregnancy. The abortion pill, on the other hand, is used to terminate an established pregnancy.

**Will taking emergency contraception affect my ability to get pregnant later on?** No, there is no evidence to suggest that using emergency contraception has any long-term impact on your fertility. These pills are designed for short-term use and work by temporarily delaying ovulation. Once the hormone leaves your system, your body’s natural cycle and ability to conceive will return to normal.

**What should I do if I get sick and throw up after taking the pill?** This is a great question because timing is important. If you vomit within two hours of taking the morning-after pill, it may not have been fully absorbed into your system. You should contact a healthcare provider or pharmacist right away to ask if you need to take another dose.

**I’ve heard that weight can impact how well the morning-after pill works. Is that true?** Some studies suggest that levonorgestrel-based emergency contraception, like Plan B, may be less effective for women over a certain weight. This is one of the most important reasons to speak with a healthcare professional. They can discuss all of your options, including other types of emergency contraception that might be more suitable for you, and ensure you get the most effective care.

**After using emergency contraception, how do I choose a regular birth control method?** Using emergency contraception is often a wake-up call to find a more reliable, long-term birth control solution. The best method is highly personal and depends on your health, lifestyle, and future plans. Talking with a women’s health expert is the perfect next step. They can walk you through all the choices, from hormonal pills and patches to long-acting options like IUDs, helping you find the one that fits your life perfectly.

## Key Takeaways

- **Time is Key for Emergency Contraception**: The morning-after pill is most effective when taken within 72 hours of unprotected sex, but it remains an option for up to five days. Acting sooner rather than later gives you the best outcome.
